Figure: Sensitivity Analysis — Experiment 2 (Threat Level: ID015 / ID017 / ID030)
==================================================================================

FILE: sensitivity_exp2.png

OVERVIEW
--------
This figure applies the same cutoff-sweep logic used in Experiment 1 to each
of the three face conditions in Experiment 2, asking whether a directional tilt
bias emerges for any face — and whether any such result is stable across the
angle cutoff space or is localised to specific boundary choices.

WHAT IS D
---------
D = mean(towards angle) − mean(away angle), computed per participant, then
aggregated via one-sample t-test against zero. A positive D means participants
tilted further on towards trials than on away trials. See figure_d_bars.txt
for full definition and balancing details.

SWEEP PARAMETERS
----------------
Min angle cutoff (y-axis):  0° to 10°, step 1°   (trial kept if angle > lo)
Max angle cutoff (x-axis): 35° to 55°, step 1°   (trial kept if angle < hi)

Total grid: 11 × 21 = 231 cutoff combinations per condition.

The N shown beneath each face image is the maximum N achieved across the full
sweep (loosest cutoffs). N at any specific grid cell will be lower.

Standard cutoff used in the main analysis: lo = 3°, hi = 40°.

FACES AND THREAT LEVELS
------------------------
ID015 — low threat face (young adult male, minimal threat cues)
         Experiment 2, Run 1 (CSV source, JSONs lost in data breach)
         and Run 2 (JSON source). Run 1 and Run 2 data combined.

ID017 — high threat face (visible tattoos, strong threat cues)
         Experiment 2, Run 2 only (JSON source).

ID030 — high threat face (visible tattoos and scarring, strong threat cues)
         Experiment 2, Run 2 only (JSON source).

LAYOUT
------
Three rows (one per face condition), three columns each:
  Col 1: Face photograph, condition label and threat level, with max-N beneath.
  Col 2: Mean D heatmap — Red = positive D; Blue = negative D; White = D ≈ 0.
           A single shared colourbar spans all six heatmaps for comparability.
  Col 3: Significance heatmap — colour encodes −log₁₀(p).
           Brighter / more yellow = more significant.
           A cyan dashed contour marks the p = .05 boundary (where present).

RESULTS — ID015, low threat (top row)
---------------------------------------
Max N = 149

The Mean D heatmap shows a mixed pattern: weakly positive D across most of the
grid but with patches of near-zero and slightly negative D, particularly at
high min cutoffs and low max cutoffs. There is no strong or consistent colour
across the grid.

The Significance heatmap is uniformly dark with no p = .05 contour. No cutoff
combination produces a statistically significant result.

At the standard cutoff: D = +0.016°, p = 0.944.

Interpretation: No directional tilt bias for the low-threat face under any
reasonable cutoff. The null result is stable across the full grid.

RESULTS — ID017, high threat (middle row)
------------------------------------------
Max N = 126

The Mean D heatmap shows a striking spatial pattern. In the upper half of the
grid (high min cutoffs, lo ≥ 5–6°), D is strongly positive (deep red),
indicating a large towards-minus-away difference when small-angle trials are
excluded. In the lower half of the grid (low min cutoffs, lo ≤ 4°), D drops
sharply and in some cells becomes negative (blue), particularly when hi > 48°.

The Significance heatmap shows a bright region concentrated at high min cutoffs
(lo ≈ 8–10°) and moderate max cutoffs (hi ≈ 35–44°), where a cyan p = .05
contour appears at the very top of the grid. This significant region is narrow
and sits at extreme min-cutoff values — far from the pre-specified standard
cutoff (lo = 3°, hi = 40°), where p = 0.258.

At the standard cutoff: D = +0.286°, p = 0.258.

Interpretation: ID017 shows a cutoff-dependent pattern. Significance appears
only when very small angles are excluded (lo ≥ 8°), substantially reducing N
and concentrating the sample on high-engagement tilts. This is not a robust
result — it is localised to a narrow and analytically aggressive region of the
cutoff space and does not include the pre-specified standard cutoff. The overall
D direction (positive) across most of the grid suggests a trend consistent with
an approach bias for high-threat faces, but the sample (N ≈ 117 at standard
cutoff) is insufficient to resolve it reliably.

RESULTS — ID030, high threat (bottom row)
------------------------------------------
Max N = 127

The Mean D heatmap shows moderate positive D across most of the grid,
particularly at moderate min cutoffs and mid-range max cutoffs. The pattern is
warmer (more positive) than ID015 but less dramatically structured than ID017.

The Significance heatmap shows elevated values across a broad band of moderate
cutoffs but does not break through the p = .05 threshold at any grid cell. No
cyan contour appears. The overall brightness is higher than ID015 but lower
than ID017 at its peak.

At the standard cutoff: D = +0.101°, p = 0.762.

Interpretation: ID030 shows a consistently positive D direction across most of
the grid but does not reach significance anywhere. As with ID017, the sample
size (N ≈ 116 at standard cutoff) limits power. The wider error bars visible
in figure_d_bars.png for ID030 (SE = 0.332) reflect high between-participant
variability for this face.

ACROSS-CONDITION COMPARISON
-----------------------------
Comparing the three rows highlights a sample-size confound that is independent
of the cutoff choice:

  ID015: N ≈ 149–236 (largest; Run 1 + Run 2 combined)
  ID017: N ≈ 117–126 (Run 2 only)
  ID030: N ≈ 116–127 (Run 2 only)

ID015 has roughly double the participants of ID017 and ID030 yet shows the
smallest and least consistent D. This is the opposite of a power artefact —
it is a genuine null result for the low-threat face. ID017 shows the largest D
values at high min cutoffs, consistent with a potential approach bias for the
high-threat face that is simply underpowered at the standard cutoff.

WHY EXP 2 DOES NOT REPLICATE EXP 1 SIGNIFICANCE
--------------------------------------------------
Three compounding factors reduce power in Exp 2 relative to Exp 1:

  1. Sample size: Exp 1 has ~577 participants per condition; Exp 2 has 116–236.
     A rough power calculation suggests Exp 2 would need approximately 400–500
     participants per face to detect an effect of the magnitude seen in Exp 1
     (D ≈ +0.24°) at 80% power.

  2. Between-participant variability: SE for Exp 2 conditions (0.225–0.332) is
     roughly twice the SE for Exp 1 Eyes Open (0.114), consistent with a more
     heterogeneous participant pool or a noisier experimental context.

  3. Face familiarity and threat calibration: The Exp 1 face (ID008) is a
     professionally photographed female face with a consistent low-threat
     signal. Exp 2 faces vary in threat cue intensity and type, which may
     produce more variable individual responses.

The sensitivity sweep confirms that the Exp 2 null results are not merely an
artefact of the standard cutoff choice — they are present across the full grid.
