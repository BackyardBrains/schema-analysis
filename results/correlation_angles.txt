Figure: User Tilt Angle vs. Tube Critical Tipping Angle (Experiment 1)
=======================================================================

OVERVIEW
--------
Each of the four tube types used in Experiment 1 has a distinct geometric
critical tipping angle — the minimum device tilt required for the liquid
surface to reach the tube rim — computed as arctan(W/H), where W and H are
the tube's pixel width and height respectively. This figure asks whether
participants tracked that difficulty level in their actual tilt responses.

Tube types and their critical angles:
  Tube [0]    24 × 130 px     critical angle = 10.5°
  Tube [1]    48 × 130 px     critical angle = 20.3°
  Tube [2]    24 ×  65 px     critical angle = 20.3°   (same ratio as [1])
  Tube [3]    48 ×  65 px     critical angle = 36.4°

Because Tubes [1] and [2] share an identical width-to-height ratio, and
therefore an identical critical angle, they are averaged into a single
x-position (20.3°) in this figure, yielding three distinct difficulty levels.

Note: the faceAngle parameter stored in the JSON (−9° to +1°) is a cosmetic
rendering offset that tilts the tube face graphic to appear to look toward the
top of the tube. It does not affect the physical tipping threshold and is not
used here.

SAMPLE
------
N = 493 participants (Experiment 1, Exp1 JSON data, valid trials only,
all tube types present). Users lacking data for all three difficulty levels
were excluded from per-user slope estimation.

LEFT PANEL — Spaghetti plot with group means
--------------------------------------------
Each thin blue line represents one participant. The line connects that
participant's mean tilt angle (across all valid towards and away trials) at
each of the three difficulty levels. The dense vertical clusters of dots show
the full distribution of per-user means at each x-position.

Group means ± 95% CI (error bars):
  Critical 10.5°  (Tube [0], 24×130):           mean user angle ≈ 12.8°
  Critical 20.3°  (Tubes [1,2], avg):            mean user angle ≈ 16.0°
  Critical 36.4°  (Tube [3], 48×65):             mean user angle ≈ 19.6°

Reference lines:
  Dotted grey (y = x): "perfect calibration" — a user whose tilt angle exactly
    equals the critical angle at every difficulty level would lie on this line.
  Dashed red: ordinary least-squares regression across all users and all
    difficulty levels (r = 0.43, p < .001, slope = 0.33).

Shaded regions:
  Green (above y = x): overshoot zone — user tilted past the true tipping point.
  Red (below y = x):   undershoot zone — user did not reach the tipping point.

Key observations:
  • At the easiest tube (10.5°) the cloud sits on or above y = x, indicating
    that most participants reach or exceed the tipping angle.
  • At medium difficulty (20.3°) the cloud drops below y = x; participants
    tilt ~4° less than required on average.
  • At the hardest tube (36.4°) the gap is severe: participants tilt ~19.6°
    on average against a threshold of 36.4°, undershooting by ~16°. Almost
    no individual user line crosses y = x at this x-position.
  • The overall regression slope (0.33 °user / °critical) confirms that
    participants adjust their tilt in the correct direction but at only about
    one-third the rate needed to track tube difficulty.

RIGHT PANEL — Per-user slope histogram
---------------------------------------
For each participant, a simple linear regression was fit to their three
(critical angle, mean user angle) data points. The resulting slope (in units
of degrees of user tilt per degree of critical angle) is shown as a histogram.

Per-user slope statistics:
  Mean slope:    0.33 °/°
  Median slope:  0.31 °/°
  SD:            ~0.34
  Slope > 0:     89% of participants
  Slope < 0:     11% of participants

A slope of 1.0 indicates perfect calibration (user matches tube difficulty
degree-for-degree). A slope of 0 means the participant tilts the same amount
regardless of tube type. Negative slopes indicate the participant tilted less
on harder tubes.

The histogram is right-skewed with the bulk of participants clustered between
0.1 and 0.6, indicating that most users do adjust — but only partially. The
minority with slopes > 1.0 over-compensate. The 11% with negative slopes
appear to anchor even more strongly on the easiest tube and do not scale up
at all.

INTERPRETATION
--------------
Participants show a statistically significant but functionally incomplete
sensitivity to tube difficulty (r = 0.43, p < .001). The pattern is
consistent with a perceptual anchoring effect: participants appear to adopt
a personal "comfortable tilt angle" — driven by visual feedback from the
dotted center line that remains visible when the tube rectangle disappears —
and adjust away from that anchor only weakly as the geometric demand
increases. This anchoring is most consequential for Tube [3] (48 × 65 px),
where the critical angle of 36.4° far exceeds the median response of ~20°,
meaning the majority of participants never physically reach the tipping point
for this tube type within the valid-trial window (3°–40°).
