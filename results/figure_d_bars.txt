Figure: D Bar Chart — Tube-Tilt Directional Bias by Condition
==============================================================

OVERVIEW
--------
This figure summarises the primary outcome measure — D — across all
conditions in Experiment 1 (Exp 1) and Experiment 2 (Exp 2). D is defined as:

    D = mean(towards angle) − mean(away angle)

where "towards" trials are those in which the tube was tilted in the direction
of the face shown on screen, and "away" trials are those in which it was tilted
in the opposite direction. D is computed per participant and then tested against
zero using a one-sample t-test. A positive D value indicates that participants
tilted further (in degrees) on towards trials than on away trials — consistent
with a face-elicited directional bias in tilt magnitude.

EXPERIMENTAL DESIGN
-------------------
Participants viewed a profile photograph of a face on one side of the screen
while tilting a virtual tube toward or away from that face. Face identity and
whether the participant's eyes were covered (Exp 1) or the threat level of the
face (Exp 2) were the key between-condition manipulations.

Exp 1 face: ID008 — young adult female, low threat rating (1.75 / 5)
Exp 2 faces: ID015 (low threat), ID017 (high threat), ID030 (high threat)

TRIAL SELECTION AND BALANCING
------------------------------
Valid trials are those where the participant's recorded tilt angle satisfies:

    3° < angle < 40°

where "angle" is the tilt magnitude in the direction of the indicated tip
(sign-corrected: raw_angle is negated for leftward tips so that positive
values always represent correct-direction tilts).

After angle-based validity marking, a single-pass cascade balance is applied:
if one trial in a towards/away pair is invalid (e.g., angle out of range),
its matched partner is also marked invalid. Partners are matched within each
participant by tube type, tip direction, and condition. This ensures that D is
computed from symmetric pairs and is not confounded by asymmetric data loss.

LAYOUT
------
Upper panel: Profile photographs of the face shown in each condition, with
participant count (N) displayed beneath each image.

Lower panel: Bar chart of mean D (degrees) per condition. Error bars show
standard error of the mean (SE). A dashed vertical line separates Exp 1 (left)
from Exp 2 (right). The horizontal reference at D = 0 represents no directional
bias. An asterisk (*) marks conditions reaching p < .05.

RESULTS
-------
Experiment 1 — ID008 (Face: young adult female)

  Eyes Open (sighted)
    N = 577 participants
    D = +0.237°,  SE = 0.114,  p = 0.039  *
    Interpretation: Participants tilted significantly further toward the face
    than away from it when the face was visible. The effect is small in absolute
    terms (~0.24°) but robust across a large sample.

  Eyes Covered (blindfold)
    N = 579 participants
    D = +0.011°,  SE = 0.110,  p = 0.921
    Interpretation: No directional bias when the same face was obscured by a
    blindfold. The near-zero D and non-significant p-value confirm that the
    effect in the Eyes Open condition is driven by visual face processing, not
    by a motor or procedural artefact.

Experiment 2 — Threat Level (face identity manipulation)

  ID015 (low threat)
    N = 236 participants
    D = +0.016°,  SE = 0.225,  p = 0.944
    Interpretation: No significant directional bias for the low-threat face.

  ID017 (high threat)
    N = 117 participants
    D = +0.286°,  SE = 0.250,  p = 0.258
    Interpretation: A numerically larger D than ID015 but non-significant,
    likely due to the substantially reduced sample size (N=117 vs N=236).
    The effect size is comparable to Exp 1 Eyes Open but underpowered.

  ID030 (high threat)
    N = 116 participants
    D = +0.101°,  SE = 0.332,  p = 0.762
    Interpretation: No significant directional bias. The wide standard error
    reflects both low N and high between-participant variability for this face.

STATISTICAL NOTE
----------------
All p-values are from two-tailed one-sample t-tests of the per-participant D
distribution against zero. No correction for multiple comparisons is applied;
conditions were specified a priori. Exp 1 and Exp 2 are analysed independently
(separate session pools, separate participant cohorts).

The N=577/579 split in Exp 1 (not exactly equal) reflects a small number of
participants whose data were valid in one eyes condition but not the other
after cascade balancing.

INTERPRETATION
--------------
The critical comparison in Exp 1 is the double dissociation: a significant
positive D for Eyes Open (p = 0.039) and a null result for Eyes Covered
(p = 0.921). This pattern — same face, same task, same tube types — rules out
alternative explanations based on task structure, response bias, or tube
geometry, and points specifically to visual face perception as the driver of
the tilt bias.

In Exp 2, all three face conditions are non-significant. The Exp 2 sample is
substantially smaller (117–236 per condition vs ~578 in Exp 1), reducing
statistical power. ID017 shows the largest numerical D (+0.286°) but would
require roughly twice the current N to approach significance at the observed
effect size. These results do not rule out a threat-modulated tilt bias; they
are consistent with an underpowered null result.
